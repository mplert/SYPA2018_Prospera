CHANNEL A: INCENTIVE EFFECTS

Previous studies have shown that Prospera increases investments in nutrition, health and schooling, of poor children. Evidence from other contexts suggests that these types of investments improve their outcomes later in life.

This portion of the study used a survey that asked a sample of graduates from Prospera about their job market outcomes to verify this intuition and assess the relationship between years of education and young adults' job market outcomes. The results indicate that one additional year of schooling is associated with a 5.3% increase in monthly salary of young adults first and current jobs, when controlling for gender, if the respondent is currently in school, years of education, if the respondent ever migrated to the US or within Mexico, and the state the respondent is from. This relationship is significant at the 1% level. There is also a positive and statistically significant relationship between years of schooling and probability that a young adult has a formal employment contract. Figure below shows our findings of our analysis for this channel.

These estimates were derived by regressing the total years of schooling completed by a young adult on their salary at their first and current job. Controls for household and young adult characteristics (household size, age, gender, currently being enrolled in school, years of schooling, whether the young adult has ever migrated within Mexico or to the US, and state fixed effects) are included. To estimate the relationship between years of school and the binary outcome of having a formal labor contract the same specification is implemented using a logit model. To interpret the results, we calculate the marginal effect of additional year of school at the means of all variables in the model. Data for this analysis come from the Encuesta Seguimiento a Jovenes de Hogares del Programa Oportunidades and Encuesta de Caracteristicas Socioeconomicas de los Hogares. More details on the analysis are available in the full report.
