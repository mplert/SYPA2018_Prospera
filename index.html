Prospering with PROSPERA
Can Conditional Cash Transfers Improve Labor Productivity in Mexico?



Mexican GDP growth and labor productivity lags behind its peer countries. Within Mexico, lower income households are particularly affected by low productivity and low wage jobs.


Prospera, formerly known as Progresa/Oportunidades, is one of Mexico's signature social programs. Implemented nationwide in 2000-2001, families receive a uniform cash transfer for nutrition, conditional on health visit attendance; scholarship transfers for each child that vary with children's grade level and gender, conditional on school attendance; and, in some cases, periodic in-kind nutritional supplements.


Previous evidence shows that Prospera has signifigant positive impacts on nutrition, health, and schooling among poor children. This project studies the program's long term impacts on employment outcomes and explores whether Prospera can be modified to improve labor productivity among young adults once they graduate from the program.


This study finds that Prospera transfers have positive effects on young adult’s wages and job quality after they graduate from the program. Youth in households that receive larger Prospera transfers have higher salaries and are more likely to be employed. Based on this, the government should consider increasing monthly transfers to all households in the program by 5-15%.



OUR FINDINGS

Survey, administrative, and qualitative data were used to investigate Prospera's long run impacts. Prospera could impact the job market outcomes of young adult graduates through three channels: incentivizing increased investment in children, providing additional income to increase in investments in children, and providing supplemental programs to prepare youth for jobs.


The study found that Channel B, the additional income from the cash transfers, has a meaningful effect on job outcomes, and that there is scope to increase these positive effects by increasing the magnitude of transfers. A 10% increase in transfers increases youth’s salary at their current job by 9.5%, and the probability youth’s current job is formal by 1.9%.


While the incentive effects of Channel A do increase investments in youth health and education, there is little scope for additional conditions to improve on these gains. Supplementary programs, though they present interesting opportunities for innovation, have yet to demonstrate meaningful results and have faced implementation challenges.



RECOMMENDATIONS

This analysis suggest that increasing transfers can meaningfully improve young adults’ salaries and likelihood of securing formal labor contracts. Based on these results, the government should consider increasing the monthly nutrition transfer to each household by 5-15%.


The increase in transfer should be made to the nutrition component of the program, so that households with children of all ages receive the same increase.

ABOUT THIS PROJECT

This website and the research findings presented here were prepared for the Second-Year Policy Analysis (SYPA) for the fulfillment of the requirements of the Master in Public Administration in International Development (MPA/ID) program at the John F. Kennedy School of Government at Harvard University. The full report can be downloaded here. Data and reference lists can be found here.
