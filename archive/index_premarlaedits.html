Prospering with PROSPERA
Can Conditional Cash Transfers Improve Labor Productivity in Mexico?



Prospera, formerly known as Progresa and Oportunidades, is one of the signature programs of the Mexican social sector. First piloted in 1997 and implemented nationwide in 2000-2001, the program provides cash and transfers to poor households, conditional on children's school attendance and participation in basic nutrition and health visits. Families receive a monthly cash transfers for nutrition that do not vary with household size, conditional on health visit attendance; monthly scholarship transfers for each child that vary with child’s grade level and gender, conditional on school attendance; annual school supply transfers for each child; and, in some cases, periodic in kind nutritional supplements.


Our research shows that Prospera transfers have positive effects on young adult’s wages and job quality after they graduate from the program. Youth in households that receive larger Prospera transfers have higher salaries and are more likely to be employed. To build on these positive effects, we recommend that the government consider increasing monthly transfers to all households in the program by 5-15%.



PROBLEM STATEMENT

Mexican GDP growth and labor productivity growth lags behind its peer countries. Within Mexico, lower income communities are particularly affected.


Many studies have shown that Prospera has achieved the goal of evening the playing field for poor children by increasing the years of school they complete and improving their health and nutrition status. Yet, whether or not these gains have translated into improvements in job outcomes for graduates remains unclear.


Our study examines the impacts of the Prospera program on labor market outcome of its youth graduates, and makes recommendations for how Prospera and its supplementary programs can be enhanced to improve the labor market outcomes of children in low-income households.

Examining each impact channel allowed us to identify which aspects of the program could be modified or enhanced to improve youth’s long run outcomes.


Our investigation found that channel B, the additional income from the cash transfers has a meaningful effect on their job outcomes, and that there is scope to increase these positive effects by increasing the magnitude of transfers. A 1% increase in transfers increases the salary youth’s current job by .95%, and the probability youth’s current job is formal by .19%.


While the incentive effects of channel A do increase investments in youth health and education, there is little scope for additional conditions to improve on these gains. Supplementary programs, though they present interesting opportunities for innovation, have yet to demonstrate meaningful results and have faced implementation challenges.



RECOMMENDATIONS

Increasing amount of monthly nutrition transfer to each household by between 5% and 15%, in real terms is can improve youth’s productivity once they join the labor force. Our findings suggest that this increase would result in meaningful improvements in youth’s salaries and in the likelihood youth will secure formal labor contracts.


The increase in transfer should be made to the nutrition component of the program, so that households with children of all ages receive the same increase.
